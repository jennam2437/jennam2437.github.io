Accessibility in a Digital Setting

Digitally, accessibility takes on a similar, yet even more complicated role. Social, and physical disabilities all come together in one website, and it is up to the developers to create a space that is accessible to all. When developing anything, from a website to a building, it is imperative that Universal Design is adapted into the project from conception. Websites such as the BBC homepage were designed specifically to be as accessible as possible from the beginning.

Making a website accessible, seems like a daunting, expensive task. There are so many people to accommodate for and guidelines to meet. The reality is, it is harder than simply ignoring it. However, by neglecting to accommodate early, you are likely to spend more time and money adding on arbitrary accommodations to your website. They will fail to help a majority of the people it was designed for and alienate an entire population from your website. Accommodate early, design your website universally, and you will be grateful for the hard work when your website is fundamentally accessible to every consumer.

It’s easy to say “accommodate early”, but it’s a lot harder to implement. When beginning a website, consider your accommodations. Who are they meant to help? Common disabilities to note are:

  • Visual disabilities

  • Mobility disabilities

  • Auditory disabilities

  • Seizure risk

  • Cognitive and learning disabilities

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are another way to guide yourself in the right direction. WCAG uses a rating system that is useful for UX and product designers to reference. Level A is the minimum acceptance threshold. It includes accessibility features and does achieve broad accessibility. It is navigable via keyboard and incorporates meaning through non-visual elements. Level AA is acceptable compliance. Most users, including disabled people, find the site usable. Photos must have alt text, there must be consist navigation elements, logical heading structure, and more. Video captions and audio descriptions are also included in this tier. The highest compliance a website could achieve is level AAA. It optimizes everything covered in the previous tiers, maximizes contrast options for the visually impaired, includes sign-language interpretation, and obeys the three-flash rule.

WCAG also provides 4 guidelines for developers to remember.

  • Perceivable: text alternatives, time-based media, captions, audio descriptions

  • Operable: keyboard accessibility, character shortcuts, navigability, input modalities

  • Understandable: readable language, consistent identification, structured headings and paragraphs, error identification, help pages

  • Robust: compatibility

It is important to remember these guidelines when designing a website. Find the right tools to help you assess how useful your accommodations are. It is useful to test your websites with a group of participants before publishing. It is also useful to test with personal accommodations in mind. How does your website work with a screen reader? Inverted colors? Larger text? Some of these features could work as the main function of the website instead of an add-on. Larger text and compatible colors help the visually impaired, but it also makes your website easier and more understandable for everyone. Accessibility is for the disabled, but it can also function in a dual role and a way to make your website better for all.
