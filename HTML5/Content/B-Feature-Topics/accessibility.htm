What is Accessibility?

In the physical world, accessibility is a measurement of how things can be used by as many people as possible. It ensures that- regardless of ability- all people are able to interact with the spaces around them. Accessibility is everywhere, though we tend to take it for granted. Wheelchair ramps, automatic doors, elevators, and signage are all examples of accommodations we see in buildings every day and have become standard.

Accessibility is not one-size-fits-all, and disabilities are unique to everyone. Accessibility must be flexible and adaptable to anyone who needs it. Disabilities come in many different forms, from physical to mental. There are different approaches to accessibility, depending on who the developer is and their intended purpose. Developers who subscribe the “Social Model” of disability recognize that it is not the wheelchair that disables the user, but the lack of accessibility in the staircase. This developer would probably take the time to redesign the traditional staircase from the ground up in a way that is both convenient and functional for the user. It places the disability onto society, and therefore also the responsibility of designing an environment in which everyone has the ability to use it. A developer who subscribes to the “Medical Model” sees the condition of the wheelchair user as the disability, not the staircase. While this is useful for diagnosing and treating disabilities, it is not conducive to functional accessibility.
